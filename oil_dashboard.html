02 Types of Crude — Light, Heavy, Sweet, Sour Fundamentals
+
// The 2×2 Crude Quality Matrix
Light Sweet
The Premium Grade
Small molecules, low sulfur. Easy to refine, naturally yields the most gasoline and jet fuel. Minimal processing required. West Texas Intermediate (WTI) and Brent crude are both in this category.
Premium pricing · $2–5 above benchmark
Light Sour
Common but Needs Treating
Small molecules but high sulfur content. Requires desulfurization (hydrotreating) before products meet fuel standards. Slightly discounted vs sweet equivalents. Common in some Russian and Middle Eastern fields.
Moderate discount · $1–3 below sweet
Heavy Sweet
Rare but Processable
Dense, viscous oil but low sulfur. Needs significant cracking to produce lighter products but avoids the sulfur headache. Relatively rare. Some Canadian synthetic crudes fall here after initial upgrading.
Moderate discount · varies by refinery
Heavy Sour
The Difficult Grade
Dense AND high sulfur. Needs both extensive cracking AND desulfurization. Only refineries specifically built for it (coking units, deep conversion) can process it efficiently. Venezuelan heavy crude, some Canadian oil sands.
Significant discount · $5–15 below WTI

// The US produces mainly light sweet (shale). OPEC produces a mix, mostly medium sour. This mismatch is why the US simultaneously exports and imports crude.

// The Petrodollar Architecture
THE DEAL (1974)
Saudi Arabia agrees to price oil exclusively in USD and recycle profits into US Treasuries. USA provides military protection.
THE EFFECT
Every country on earth needs USD to buy oil. This creates permanent structural demand for dollars — underwriting US financial dominance globally.
THE STRESS (NOW)
China pushing yuan-denominated oil contracts with Gulf states. Saudi Arabia is hedging. A successful shift would be the most significant blow to USD dominance in 50 years.

06 The Investment Theses — Multiple Plays From One Macro Shift Strategy
+
// Thesis Map — Who Benefits From Peak Oil Demand
⚗️
Specialty Chemicals & Petrochemicals
As oil-as-fuel demand drops, feedstock (naphtha, ethane) gets cheaper. Chemical companies converting cheap inputs into high-margin engineered materials are the leveraged play. SABIC, LyondellBasell, Dow.
Bullish · 2030–2045 window
🧪
Advanced Polymers & Engineered Materials
Specialty polymers for EVs, aerospace, medical devices. Low regulatory risk vs commodity plastics. High margin, IP-protected, demand growing independently of energy transition direction.
Bullish · structural tailwind
🛢️
Gulf State Downstream Expansion
Saudi Arabia (SABIC/Aramco), UAE (ADNOC) building massive domestic petrochemical capacity. They're racing to capture the chemicals margin themselves rather than selling cheap crude upstream. Watch their capex.
Watching · strategic pivot
♻️
Bio-Based Plastics (Near Term)
If oil gets cheap in the price war window, bio-plastics lose their cost competitiveness. They need expensive oil to be viable at scale. The transition thesis here depends entirely on price assumptions going the right way.
Cautious · headwind from cheap oil
⚡
Energy Transition Infrastructure
Solar, wind, grid storage, EV charging infrastructure. Accelerated as oil demand peaks. Ironically, solar panels and EV batteries require significant petrochemical inputs — the old and new economy are entangled.
Bullish · secular tailwind
🔬
Silicone & Silicon-Based Materials
Not petroleum-derived — follows EV, solar, and electronics demand instead. Completely separate thesis from oil pricing. Demand grows as oil declines. Wacker Chemie, Shin-Etsu Chemical are key names.
Structural · unrelated to oil price
🏭
Cheap-Input Manufacturing
If oil floods the market in the price war window (2030s–2040s), energy-intensive manufacturing gets a structural cost advantage. Petrochemical-dependent goods become cheaper to produce. Industrial manufacturers win.
Bullish · medium-term window
⛽
Upstream E&P (Expensive Oil)
US shale, Canadian oil sands, North Sea — high cost, high capital intensity, first to be stranded. EV adoption + potential price war from OPEC = double squeeze. Long-term capex into these assets looks increasingly misallocated.
Bearish · stranded asset risk
